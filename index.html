Studying how democracy holds up under pressure — one dataset at a time.

I'm a PhD student in Political Science, working at the intersection of comparative democratization, party politics, and African political systems. My research leans on large-N cross-national data to ask questions that are easy to state and hard to answer cleanly.

View research Download CV

I hold a BA in Political Science (Economics minor) from the University of Education, Winneba, Ghana, and an MA in Political Science from Ohio University, where I graduated with a 3.958 GPA. I began my PhD in the Department of Political Science at UWM in Fall 2025, and expect to complete the program in 2031.

My work combines quantitative methods — panel models, fixed-effects estimation, power analysis in R and SPSS — with substantive questions about how democratic institutions function, or fail to, across very different political contexts. Before returning to political science research full-time, I worked as a Field Organizer for the Democratic Party of Wisconsin, running grassroots voter-contact programs across the state.

Much of my current research examines candidate selection, party factionalism, and rural political incorporation across Sub-Saharan Africa and beyond, using data from V-Dem, V-Party, and the Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS). I'm Ghanaian, and I speak English and Twi.

Under Review
Do Primaries Pay? Candidate Selection Methods and Electoral Performance Across Competitive Democracies
Submitted to Political Studies Review · 2026
A large-N comparative study of primary-style candidate selection across roughly 115 democracies using V-Party and V-Dem data. Once fixed effects are introduced, the expected "primary bonus" in electoral performance largely disappears — a null result that speaks to ongoing debates about intra-party democracy and its electoral payoff.
